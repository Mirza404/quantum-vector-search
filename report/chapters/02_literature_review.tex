\section{Overview}
\label{sec:lit:overview}

This chapter reviews the prior work the study builds on, in the order the
methodology uses it. Section~\ref{sec:lit:classical} covers classical vector
search, from the exact scan to the approximate graph indexes that are the
production standard and the strongest classical baseline a quantum method must
beat. Section~\ref{sec:lit:embeddings} covers the cross-modal embeddings that
place text and images in one comparable space. Section~\ref{sec:lit:quantum}
covers the two quantum primitives relevant to similarity search, Grover's search
and the swap test, and the data-loading cost their asymptotic figures omit.
Section~\ref{sec:lit:hybrid} reviews the retrieve-and-rerank cascade that
motivates pairing a classical index with a quantum scorer. The aim throughout is
to give a reader who is not a quantum specialist just enough background to follow
the methodology.

\section{Classical Vector Search}
\label{sec:lit:classical}

% Cover, in order of complexity: exact brute force, FAISS flat indices,
% approximate-nearest-neighbour graphs (HNSW). Frame HNSW as the production
% standard - it is the most important comparison point on slide 11 of the
% midterm deck.

The baseline for vector search is an exact scan: the query vector is compared
against every stored vector, and the closest are returned. This guarantees the
correct nearest neighbours but costs $O(N)$ comparisons per query, which becomes
the bottleneck as a collection grows. FAISS is a widely used library that
performs this exact search efficiently by exploiting the vectorised instructions
of modern processors, and extends it to very large
collections~\cite{johnson2019faiss}. To fit very large collections in memory,
the stored vectors are often compressed: product quantization splits each vector
into subvectors and quantizes them separately~\cite{jegou2011pq}, and the
inverted multi-index organises the compressed codes for fast
lookup~\cite{babenko2015multiindex}. When exact search is too slow, approximate
nearest-neighbour methods accept a small loss in recall for a large gain in
speed. These span several families: hashing schemes such as locality-sensitive
hashing~\cite{datar2004lsh}, learned quantization such as
ScaNN~\cite{guo2020scann}, and graph-based indexes that connect each vector to a
set of neighbours~\cite{fu2019nsg,subramanya2019diskann}, with standardised
benchmarks comparing them on the recall-versus-speed
trade-off~\cite{aumuller2020annbenchmarks}. The most widely used of these is the
HNSW graph, which links each vector to a set of near neighbours across several
layers and answers a query by greedily descending the graph, reaching a near
neighbour in roughly $O(\log N)$ steps~\cite{malkov2020hnsw}. HNSW is the kind of
fast, approximate index a
practical retrieval system would choose before considering a quantum
alternative, so it is the relevant point of comparison: a quantum search that
hopes to be useful must improve on a classical method that is already sublinear,
not only on a simple linear scan.

\section{Cross-Modal Embeddings}
\label{sec:lit:embeddings}

% One short subsection on CLIP. Explain contrastive learning at a high level
% and why text and image embeddings end up in the same metric space.

Cross-modal retrieval depends on embedding different kinds of media into a
common space. Modern encoders are built on the Transformer
architecture~\cite{vaswani2017attention}: images are encoded by a Vision
Transformer that treats image patches as tokens~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit}, and
text by a bidirectional language model in the style of
BERT~\cite{devlin2019bert}. CLIP brings the two together for text and images by
training a text encoder and an image encoder jointly, so that an image and its
caption map to nearby vectors while mismatched pairs are pushed
apart~\cite{radford2021clip}. The same contrastive idea underlies sentence
embeddings~\cite{reimers2019sentencebert} and has been scaled to noisier
web-sized data by later models such as ALIGN~\cite{jia2021align}. A common CLIP
variant pairs a Vision Transformer image encoder (ViT-B/32) with a text encoder
and produces 512-dimensional embeddings, in which the cosine similarity between a
text vector and an image vector measures their relevance. This reduces
text-to-image retrieval to ordinary nearest-neighbour search in a single vector
space, which is what makes a quantum similarity primitive applicable to the
cross-modal task at all.

\section{Quantum Similarity Primitives}
\label{sec:lit:quantum}

% Swap test (Buhrman et al.), amplitude encoding state preparation, Grover.
% Be honest about what is "speedup" and what is "amortised state preparation
% cost" - the qRAM caveat goes here, not in the methodology.

Two quantum building blocks are relevant to similarity search; both are
described in standard form by Nielsen and Chuang~\cite{nielsen2010quantum}.
Grover's algorithm searches an unstructured collection of $N$ items for one that
satisfies a condition checked by an oracle, and it does so with about $\lfloor
(\pi/4)\sqrt{N} \rfloor$ oracle queries, a quadratic improvement on the $N$
checks a classical scan requires~\cite{grover1996}; it is the best-known instance
of the more general amplitude-amplification framework~\cite{brassard2002amplitude}.
The swap test, introduced in work on quantum
fingerprinting, compares two quantum states by placing an ancilla qubit in
superposition, conditionally swapping the two states, and measuring the ancilla;
the probability of reading the ancilla as zero is $(1 + |\langle\psi|\phi\rangle|^2)/2$,
so the test recovers the squared overlap of the states and with it a similarity
score~\cite{buhrman2001swaptest}.

Both techniques assume the data is already present as quantum states, and this
assumption carries the cost that the asymptotic figures hide. Encoding $N$
vectors into a quantum state, or building an oracle that recognises the closest
of them, requires reading all $N$ items, and the state-preparation circuits that
load a classical vector as amplitudes grow in depth with the vector
dimension~\cite{mottonen2005statprep,plesch2011statprep}. A qRAM has been
proposed to supply data items in superposition~\cite{giovannetti2008qram}, but no
such device exists at usable scale, and if the data must instead be loaded one
item at a time, the $O(N)$ preparation cost cancels Grover's quadratic advantage
at the level of the whole system~\cite{aaronson2015readfine}. The same caveat
applies to quantum linear-algebra routines such as the HHL algorithm, whose
exponential speedup is contingent on efficient state preparation and
readout~\cite{harrow2009hhl}. These constraints are sharpest on the noisy
intermediate-scale (NISQ) devices available today~\cite{preskill2018nisq}.
Hoefler, H\"{a}ner, and Troyer make the same practical point more broadly:
quantum computers are most promising for small-data problems with very large
computation, while big-data search is limited by input and output
movement~\cite{hoefler2023practicality}.
The distinction between Grover's scaling as a property of the search step alone
and the data-loading cost that figure omits is the central tension the prior
literature raises for quantum vector search.

\section{Hybrid Classical--Quantum Architectures}
\label{sec:lit:hybrid}

% Brief discussion of recent hybrid retrieval pipelines that use a classical
% prefilter and a quantum reranker. Frame our hybrid_hnsw_swap_test engine as
% an instance of this pattern.

The retrieve-and-rerank cascade is a standard pattern in information retrieval: a
fast, inexpensive first stage selects a small set of candidates from the whole
collection, and a slower but more accurate second stage reorders only those
candidates. The pattern is widely used in modern neural retrieval, where a dense
retriever shortlists passages~\cite{karpukhin2020dpr} and a heavier model
re-ranks them~\cite{nogueira2019passagererank}. The cost of the expensive stage
is then bounded by the size of the shortlist rather than by the size of the
collection. This pattern is a natural fit for near-term quantum hardware. Quantum
machine learning has been proposed for a range of tasks, including clustering and
classification~\cite{lloyd2013quantum,rebentrost2014qsvm}, nearest-neighbour
methods~\cite{wiebe2015nearestneighbor}, and kernel methods that embed data in a
quantum feature space~\cite{schuld2019featurehilbert,havlicek2019supervised,lloyd2020embeddings,schuld2021kernelmethods},
often realised as variational circuits~\cite{cerezo2021variational}. In practice
it remains constrained by considerable hardware and software
challenges~\cite{biamonte2017qml,wittek2014qml}, so a practical near-term design
keeps the global, data-bound work on classical hardware and reserves the quantum
processor for a small, structured subproblem.
Confining the quantum scorer to a fixed-size shortlist keeps the number of
circuit executions independent of the collection size and places any estimation
error where it can only reorder an already-relevant shortlist rather than miss a
relevant item entirely. The hybrid engine evaluated in this report is an instance
of this cascade; its construction and results are described in
Chapters~\ref{ch:methodology} and~\ref{ch:results}.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:lit:summary}

The prior work reviewed here frames the comparison the rest of the report carries
out. Classical vector search already reaches sublinear query cost through
approximate graph indexes, so HNSW, not the linear scan, is the baseline a
quantum method must beat. Cross-modal embeddings such as CLIP reduce text-to-image
retrieval to nearest-neighbour search in one space, which is what lets a quantum
similarity primitive apply to the task. Grover's search and the swap test offer,
respectively, a quadratic reduction in oracle queries and a measurement-based
overlap estimate, but both assume the data is already encoded as quantum states,
and the cost of that encoding is what the asymptotic figures omit. The
retrieve-and-rerank cascade suggests the most plausible near-term role for a
quantum scorer: a bounded second stage behind a classical first stage. These
threads motivate the methodology of the next chapter.
