\chapter{CONCLUSION}
\label{ch:conclusion}

% Summarise findings, highlight contributions, point to future work.
% Target length: ~300-500 words. No new results may appear here.

This study built a single framework in which classical, quantum, and hybrid
engines answer the same cross-modal queries over the same embeddings, and used it
to measure where quantum similarity search helps. The findings are consistent and,
in places, sobering. Retrieval accuracy was governed by the embedding dimension
rather than by whether an engine was quantum: the swap test reproduced classical
accuracy once given a sufficient measurement budget (RQ1), and our Grover
implementation reproduced its theoretical $\lfloor (\pi/4)\sqrt{N} \rfloor$
oracle-call count exactly at the size we tested (RQ2). No engine that performed
its search quantumly outperformed the classical HNSW baseline on this dataset;
the only quantum-containing engine to match it, the hybrid, did so by delegating
the search to the classical index (RQ3). The structural reason is that vector
retrieval is dominated by reading the dataset, a data-bound task, whereas quantum
advantage concentrates on compute-bound problems over small inputs, so cross-modal
search is not a natural fit for quantum methods even setting hardware maturity
aside (RQ4).

This work delivers four things. The first is a reproducible benchmarking framework
that runs seven engines, classical, quantum, and hybrid, behind one interface and
records every result in a database. The second is an empirical verification of
textbook quantum behaviour on a real retrieval task: the swap test's convergence
to the classical ranking, and Grover's oracle-call scaling. The third is a web
application that displays the engines' rankings side by side, making the
comparison inspectable rather than abstract. The fourth, and the one we consider
most important, is the honest framing itself: stating plainly where quantum search
does not help, and why, is a contribution in a setting where that question is
often left unasked.

Several directions would extend the study. A larger dataset, drawn from the full
Flickr30k set or an ImageNet subset, would exercise the HNSW approximation that
twenty vectors leave untested and give the accuracy figures more statistical
weight. A wider dimension sweep, and a study of variational state-preparation
circuits in place of the hardcoded oracle, would probe the quantum engines closer
to their intended use. Comparing against a production vector store such as
pgvector's HNSW would add a third classical baseline, and a characterisation of
noise on real quantum hardware, beyond the single validation run reported here,
would show how the simulated behaviour degrades on present-day devices.
