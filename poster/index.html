Quantum-Enhanced Multi-Modal Vector Search

A Hybrid Database Web App: Classical vs. Quantum Similarity

Elhan Mahmutovic · Adna Kikanovic · Adnan Musanovic · Mirza Abdulahovic

Mentor: Assist. Prof. Dr. Ali Abd Almisreb

Faculty of Engineering and Natural Sciences · International University of Sarajevo · 2026

We build a text-to-image search system that runs the same query through eight engines — three classical (brute force, FAISS flat, FAISS HNSW) and five quantum (swap test, Grover, Grover with quantum state preparation, a HNSW + swap-test hybrid, and the hybrid run on IBM hardware) — and compare them on accuracy (MRR) and quantum resource cost (qubits, circuit depth, oracle calls). The investigation is framed honestly: we measure where quantum search stands today, not where the marketing says it stands.

1. Problem & Research Questions

  1. Accuracy. Does the swap-test reproduce classical cosine ranking, and at what shot budget?
  2. Scaling. Does our Grover implementation match the ⌊π√N ÷ 4⌋ oracle-call curve on real circuits?
  3. Practicality. Under what conditions, if any, can a quantum engine beat a production HNSW baseline?

2. Why This Matters

Vector search is the backbone of modern ML — recommendation, retrieval, semantic search. Quantum algorithms promise asymptotic speedups, but few experiments measure them on real embeddings with a fair classical baseline. We build that comparison now, so the framework is ready when hardware matures.

We are not claiming quantum is faster. Classical wins today. The contribution is the honest, reproducible measurement.

3. Methodology

5. Evaluation Metrics

Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR). Average of 1 / rank of first relevant result across queries. Higher is better; 1.0 means always first.

Oracle calls. Number of similarity comparisons per query. N for exact classical and swap test; ⌊π√N ÷ 4⌋ for Grover. Hardware-independent — the only valid cross-engine scaling metric.

Why we do not compare wall-clock time across engines: quantum engines run on a simulator that tracks 2k complex amplitudes on a classical CPU. The wall-clock reflects simulation overhead, not real quantum runtime.

7. The Honest Quantum Picture

Grover's √N speedup is real on the search step alone. The catch is loading N vectors into superposition without qRAM (which does not exist) costs O(N) — cancelling the speedup at the system level.

Even with hypothetical perfect qRAM, classical HNSW achieves O(log N), which grows slower than O(√N). At N = 106, HNSW needs ~20 graph hops while Grover needs ~785 oracle calls. HNSW wins on a laptop today.

8. Conclusion & Future Work

  • Reproducible benchmarking infrastructure for classical vs. quantum vector search, controlled by one YAML file.
  • Empirical verification that the swap-test recovers cosine ranking and Grover hits √N oracle scaling.
  • Honest analysis of why quantum vector search is not competitive today — and what would have to change.

Next: larger dataset, full 512-dim CLIP, IBM hardware noise study, pgvector HNSW as a third classical baseline.
